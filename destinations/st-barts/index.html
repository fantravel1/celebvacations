About St. Barts

A tiny volcanic island of just 8 square miles, St. Barts punches astronomically above its weight as a celebrity destination. This French-speaking paradise has been the winter playground of billionaires and A-listers since the 1950s, when David Rockefeller first put it on the jet-set map.

What sets St. Barts apart is its unique combination of Caribbean natural beauty and French cultural refinement. The island maintains strict building codes—no structure can exceed the height of a palm tree—ensuring an unspoiled landscape. There are no all-inclusive resorts, no chain hotels, and no cruise ship ports. Entry is either by small aircraft (the dramatic runway landing is legendary) or by boat, adding to the exclusive atmosphere.

The winter season transforms the island into a floating exhibition of the world's most impressive superyachts. The harbor at Gustavia fills with vessels belonging to tech billionaires, fashion moguls, and entertainment royalty. New Year's Eve on St. Barts is considered the most exclusive celebration on Earth, with private parties that cost millions to host.

The New Year's Phenomenon

St. Barts at New Year's is unlike anywhere else on Earth. The harbor fills with mega-yachts worth billions combined. Private jets queue at the tiny airport. Hotel rooms that cost $1,000 per night in summer fetch $10,000+. The celebrations center around exclusive parties at villas and yachts, with guest lists that read like a Who's Who of global celebrity.

Frequently Asked Questions

St. Barts attracts the highest concentration of celebrity visitors anywhere in the Caribbean. Regular visitors include Leonardo DiCaprio, Beyonce & Jay-Z, the Kardashian family, Simon Cowell, Marc Jacobs, Roman Abramovich, Paul McCartney, and numerous supermodels and fashion industry figures. The island is particularly famous for its New Year's Eve celebrations, which draw an extraordinary roster of A-listers.

The peak celebrity season runs from mid-December through early April, with Christmas and New Year's being the absolute pinnacle—and most expensive time. Late November and early December offer excellent weather with fewer crowds. Summer (June-August) is quieter and more affordable, with some restaurants and hotels closing. Hurricane season runs June through November, though St. Barts rarely sees direct hits.

There are no direct flights from the US or Europe. Most visitors fly into St. Maarten (SXM) and then take a small 10-minute propeller plane or ferry to St. Barts. Private jets can land at Gustaf III Airport, though the short runway (only 650 meters) requires specially certified pilots. Many celebrities arrive by superyacht, anchoring in Gustavia harbor.
